About Matcha

Learn about the history, origins, and how matcha is traditionally made.

What is Matcha?

Matcha is a finely ground powder made from specially cultivated green tea leaves. It’s celebrated for its vibrant green color, distinct umami flavor, and impressive health-boosting properties. More than just a drink, matcha carries deep cultural significance, especially in Japan, where it plays a central role in the traditional tea ceremony.

Unlike steeped green tea, where you brew and discard the leaves, matcha is consumed whole, giving it a stronger nutritional profile and richer flavor.

The Origin of Matcha

While matcha is most closely associated with Japan, its history actually began in 12th-century China. Buddhist monks ground tea leaves into fine powder and whisked them into hot water to create a meditative drink known as “beaten tea.”

Japanese monk Myoan Eisai introduced this practice to Japan after studying in China. He recognized matcha’s calming yet invigorating properties, ideal for meditation. Over time, matcha became a cherished part of Zen Buddhist rituals and was later embraced by the samurai class for its mental clarity and physical endurance benefits. Eventually, matcha evolved into the centerpiece of the Japanese tea ceremony — a beautiful tradition that honors harmony, respect, purity, and tranquility.

How to Make Matcha

Step 1

Sift 1–2 teaspoons of matcha powder into a bowl using a fine sieve. This helps remove clumps and makes the matcha smoother when mixed.

Step 2

Pour a small amount of hot water (about 70–80°C, not boiling) into the bowl. Using water that is too hot can make the matcha taste bitter.

Step 3

Use a bamboo whisk (chasen) or regular whisk to mix the matcha in a zig-zag “M” motion until it becomes smooth and slightly frothy on top.

Step 4

Drink it as is for traditional matcha, or add milk and sweetener to make a matcha latte. Stir gently and enjoy!
